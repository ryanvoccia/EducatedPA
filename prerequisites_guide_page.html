Understanding PA School Prerequisites: A State-by-State Guide

Published on June 30, 2025 • 4 min read

Navigating PA school prerequisites can feel overwhelming, especially when requirements vary significantly between programs and states. Unlike medical school, where prerequisites are relatively standardized, PA programs have diverse requirements that reflect their unique educational philosophies and regional healthcare needs.

Understanding these variations and planning your academic path strategically can save you time, money, and stress while ensuring you're competitive for your target programs.

Core Prerequisites: The Universal Requirements

Science Foundation Courses

Nearly all PA programs require these fundamental science courses:

General Chemistry (1-2 semesters)

  • Usually includes lab components
  • Organic chemistry may be preferred but isn't always required
  • Some programs accept chemistry for allied health professionals

Biology (1-2 semesters)

  • General biology with lab
  • Some programs specify cell biology or molecular biology
  • Advanced courses like genetics may be preferred

Anatomy and Physiology (2 semesters)

  • Almost universally required
  • Must include lab components
  • Some programs accept integrated A&P courses
  • Cadaver-based anatomy may be preferred at competitive programs

Microbiology (1 semester)

  • Required by most programs
  • Lab component typically required
  • Medical microbiology preferred over general microbiology

Mathematics Requirements

Statistics (1 semester)

  • Increasingly required by programs
  • Some accept biostatistics or research methods courses
  • Online courses may not be accepted by all programs

College Algebra or Higher

  • Many programs require at least college-level math
  • Some prefer calculus or higher-level mathematics

Additional Common Requirements

Psychology (1-2 semesters)

  • General psychology almost always required
  • Some programs specify abnormal, developmental, or social psychology

English/Communications

  • College-level English composition
  • Some programs require speech or communications courses

Regional and State Variations

Northeast Programs

Many northeastern programs emphasize research and often prefer:

  • Biochemistry in addition to general chemistry
  • Higher-level math including calculus
  • Research experience or research methods courses
  • Genetics as a preferred course

Southeast Programs

Programs in the Southeast often focus on primary care and may require:

  • Medical terminology courses
  • Nutrition or health-related electives
  • Cultural competency or diversity-focused courses

Western Programs

West Coast programs frequently emphasize holistic care and may prefer:

  • Public health coursework
  • Spanish language proficiency
  • Community health or social work background

Midwest Programs

Many Midwest programs maintain traditional requirements but increasingly add:

  • Healthcare ethics coursework
  • Informatics or technology-related courses
  • Rural health experience or understanding

Grade Requirements and Competitiveness

Minimum vs. Competitive GPAs

While programs may list minimum GPAs of 3.0, competitive applicants typically need:

  • Overall GPA: 3.5 or higher
  • Science GPA: 3.4 or higher
  • Recent coursework: Strong performance in recent years

Grade Currency

Most programs require prerequisites completed within:

  • 5-7 years for science courses
  • 10 years for non-science requirements
  • Some programs make exceptions for healthcare professionals with relevant experience

Retaking Courses

When to Retake:

  • Grades below C in required courses
  • Multiple C grades in science courses
  • Poor performance that doesn't reflect current abilities

Strategic Retaking:

  • Focus on courses where improvement will have the biggest GPA impact
  • Consider taking advanced courses in areas of weakness
  • Some programs average original and retake grades

Course Planning Strategies

For Career Changers

If you're switching from a non-science background:

Year 1-2: Foundation Building

  • Start with general chemistry and biology
  • Add math and psychology courses
  • Consider taking prerequisite courses at community colleges for cost savings

Year 2-3: Advanced Sciences

  • Complete anatomy & physiology and microbiology
  • Add statistics and any remaining requirements
  • Begin patient care experience during this phase

For Recent Graduates

If you're a recent graduate missing some requirements:

Post-Bacc Programs

  • Formal post-baccalaureate programs for health professions
  • Provides structured curriculum and academic support
  • Often includes MCAT/GRE preparation and application guidance

DIY Approach

  • Take missing courses at local colleges or universities
  • Online options for some non-lab courses
  • Consider summer intensives for rapid completion

For Current Healthcare Workers

If you're already working in healthcare:

Evening/Weekend Programs

  • Many colleges offer prerequisite courses designed for working professionals
  • Online lecture components with weekend labs
  • Accelerated summer courses

Employer Support

  • Check if your employer offers tuition assistance
  • Some hospitals partner with local colleges for employee education

Special Considerations

Online Coursework

Generally Accepted:

  • Psychology courses
  • Statistics (verify with individual programs)
  • English and communications
  • Math courses

Rarely Accepted:

  • Laboratory science courses
  • Anatomy and physiology
  • Microbiology with lab components

International Coursework

  • Must be evaluated by approved agencies (WES, ECE)
  • Science courses may need to be repeated in the US
  • Some programs require all prerequisites completed at US institutions

Advanced Placement (AP) Credits

  • Some programs accept AP credits for prerequisites
  • Others require college-level coursework regardless of AP scores
  • Verify individual program policies before relying on AP credits

Program-Specific Research Strategies

Essential Questions to Ask

  1. What are the exact prerequisite requirements?
  2. Are there preferred additional courses?
  3. What is the average GPA of accepted students?
  4. How recent must science courses be?
  5. Are online courses accepted?

Where to Find Information

  • Program websites: Always the primary source
  • CASPA program profiles: Standardized information
  • Information sessions: Direct access to admissions staff
  • Current students: Real-world insights about competitiveness

Financial Planning for Prerequisites

Cost-Effective Strategies

Community Colleges

  • Significantly lower cost per credit hour
  • Often have excellent science programs
  • Credits transfer to most four-year institutions

In-State vs. Out-of-State

  • Establish residency before taking expensive courses
  • Consider online options from your home state

Summer Programs

  • Intensive courses can accelerate completion
  • May be more expensive per credit but save time

Hidden Costs

  • Lab fees for science courses
  • Technology fees for online courses
  • Application fees for multiple programs with different requirements
  • Course retakes if grades aren't competitive

Timeline Planning

2-3 Years Before Application

  • Complete basic sciences and math requirements
  • Begin gaining patient care experience
  • Research target programs thoroughly

1-2 Years Before Application

  • Finish remaining prerequisites
  • Retake courses if necessary
  • Strengthen weak areas with additional coursework

Application Year

  • Focus on maintaining strong grades in any remaining courses
  • Avoid taking challenging prerequisites during application season
  • Consider gap year if prerequisites aren't complete

Red Flags to Avoid

Academic Mistakes

  • Taking too many science courses simultaneously - Can hurt your GPA
  • Ignoring grade trends - Admissions committees notice improvement or decline
  • Focusing only on minimums - Competitive programs expect excellence

Planning Errors

  • Not researching individual program requirements - Generic planning isn't sufficient
  • Leaving prerequisites until the last minute - Rushed completion often results in poor grades
  • Ignoring course sequencing - Some courses have prerequisites of their own

Conclusion

Successfully navigating PA school prerequisites requires careful planning, thorough research, and strategic decision-making. While the variety of requirements across programs can seem daunting, understanding these differences allows you to make informed choices about which programs align with your background and goals.

Remember that prerequisites are just the beginning—they're designed to ensure you have the foundational knowledge needed to succeed in PA school's rigorous curriculum. Approach them not as obstacles to overcome, but as opportunities to build the scientific foundation that will serve you throughout your career as a physician assistant.

Start your research early, plan strategically, and don't hesitate to contact programs directly with specific questions. The time you invest in thorough prerequisite planning will pay dividends in your application success and academic preparedness.
